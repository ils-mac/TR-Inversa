# Siendo Simp√°tico en La Traducci√≥n

Last week we were introduced to the concept of being simp√°tico en la traducci√≥n in [the interview of Mexican literary translator Dr. George Henson](../semana13/entrevista-gHenson-traductorLiterario-esMX-enUS.md).

This week you'll be translating the literature you selected for your literary project last week. In your translation, one of goals will be authenticity. This authenticity is achieved by either being a part of the discourse community with whom you are communicating, or by replicating the discourse of a community when you are an outsider from it. The idea of "being simpatico" is that when you're an insider who is translating that lends credibility and authenticity to your work. Here, we're going to consider some important discussions to form our own opinions about whether one needs to be "simpatico" to translate.

The focus on this page is Lawrence Venuti's chapter "Simpatico" from *The Translator's Invisibility*.

## Terminology Note

How did simpatico as a term for this concept of authenticity come to be terminologized in the field of translation?

According to Lawrence Venuti's "Simpactico" chapter that we discuss in this lesson:

> The translator works better when he and the author are simpatico, said my friend, and by this he meant not just "agreeable," or "congenial," meanings which this Italian word is
often used to signify, but also "possessing an underlying sympathy" (237). The translator
should not merely get along with the author, not merely find him likeable; there
should also be an identity between them.

## Lawrence Venuti on Being Simpatico

### Activities & Discussion Questions

#### Key Terminology

1. Throughout the text, Venuti refers to his translations of the experimentalist poetry of Milo De Angelis from Italian to English as "interpretive translation"? What distinction is Venuti trying to make about his translations in comparison to conventional translations?
2. In the chapter, Venuti describes translation as: interpretation, transformation, mistification. What similar metaphors does Venuti use for translation, and what do those metaphors convey?
3. In approaching cornerstone ideas in writing and translations, Venuti uses phrases like "abusive fidelity" and "kidnapping, and his translation of the poem "The Train Corridor" uses the words plagiary and resemblance. What does this terminology convey about his thoughts on the authenticity and authority of the translation.
4. Understanding that both the author he wanted to translate and the style in which he would translate wouldn't be acceptable to the mainstream, Venuti identifies "resistance" as his translation strategy. What pulls should translators try to resist in translation? Why is translating without a broader audience than the translator her- or himself as the reader considered to be resistance?
5. Venuti refers to his resistant translations as an act of deterritorialization of the English language in writing, and the act of reading by the audience as reterritorialization of the English language. How should we understand "deterritorialization" and "reterritorialization"?

#### Discussion 

He's talking about all of the research - correspondence with the author, understanding of philosophies like Nietzsche and Heidegger and Lacan to which the author subscribed, a careful process of drafting and weighing options to get what is ultimately a translation with bright spots and limitations that ultimately represents the translator's understanding of and work on the text. Here, the translator's work is just that, weighing word choice, navigating what two languages can and can't do, navigating the idiolect of a specific author...

What does this mean to think of translation as resistence? He knew that this author wouldn't sell, and without readers has to ask himself what is the point of translation? Does resistance become the point? Doing the work for oneself? Doing the work for the art of it? He was right about sellability - his work is often rejected for the strangeness it maintains.

The second poem of his is interesting in its quarrel between two lovers. What about applying this poem to the plagiary of translation?

## Conclusions

Just as claims about the ability to achieve 100% accuracy in translation minimalize the skill and strategy involved in producing a translation that successfully navigates the losses and gains that are inherent when rendering content from one language to another, relying on cultural affinity to explain what makes a translation "authentic" does the same.

Still, cultrual affinity helps, or at least striving to becoming an insider within the the discourse community for whom you are translating, or working with in-culture experts to ensure the translation is culturally correct.
